Issues with First Quantization Quantum Mechanics on Curved Space-times

During my senior year at Reed I worked with Prof. Joel Franklin on a thesis project. It started as a humble attempt to describe the gravitational interaction of photons that behaved according to the quantum mechanical wave-function for single photons (see here) and ended with an investigation of the reasons for the absence of bound states when treating the Dirac equation around black holes with an event horizon. Along the way I studied a variety of tools and topics in math and physics including, among others, the spectral theory of differential operators, the RAGE and ergodic theorems (thanks to Prof. Tom Wieting), self-adjoint extensions, spinors, the BTZ black hole, the tetrad formalism, gravitational waves, Hawking radiation, and Chandrasekhar separation.

The thesis at Reed is supposed to be accessible to one’s peers; that is, fellow senior undergraduates. Indeed, most incoming physics seniors (including myself) peruse theses from years past for ideas and inspiration in the search for a thesis topic. Joel helped me realize that a very compelling story could be told with some of the work I had done throughout the year: a story about a search for bound states. For the sake of the narrative there is much that I did throughout the year that is not contained in the final document. The tone throughout, but especially in the introduction and conclusion, is very casual and is meant to set the stage for the story. This thesis is not structured so much as a presentation of research findings as it is a narrative of intellectual discovery and struggle. My hope is that by providing the undergraduate reader with an understanding of the reasons why I chose to take a certain path through the space of possible research programs they will be more engaged and will come away with an understanding of not only why the Dirac equation does not admit bound states in space-times with a horizon, but also what it means to do research in theoretical physics. It is also my hope that readers with a more advanced background will find content of interest in this thesis, as several intriguing questions and points are raised, including: equivalent notions of bound states, co-ordinate invariant spectral properties of differential operators, and why closed geodesics are lost when the Dirac equation is introduced.

This attempt at presenting material that most would consider abstruse in an engaging, and hopefully different, manner has also awakened in me a desire to become a better writer and communicator of science.
