Simulating Quantum Algorithms

TL;DR: Check this out.

This project began at the 2016 Wolfram Summer Science School, where I was a participant. We had been told to arrive with a project idea in mind, and having just finished working my way through Maximilian Schlosshauer’s fantastic book Decoherence and the Quantum-to-Classical Transition, I naturally had the idea of simulating the decoherence of some quantum system, and perhaps demonstrating how the decohering timescale changes for systems of different size. My thoughts on this matter were also stimulated by a very interesting, but ultimately dissapointing, talk that Dr. Michael Kellman gave at Reed in the Fall of 2015 where he presented the results of this paper. While he didn’t talk about decoherence, his talk inspired in me an interest in simulating quantum systems, and my fascination with Max’s book demanded a need to know more about decoherence (which hadn’t been mentioned at all in my undergraduate quantum courses).

However, the moment I entered the room for my project meeting with Dr. Stephen Wolfram, he already had a different idea in mind. The idea was to build functionality into Mathematica for easily building and simulating quantum algorithms that was powerful enough for researchers to use and simple enough to be used as an educational tool. At the time I wasn’t aware that only a month earlier IBM had launched their Quantum Experience platform, but I’m sure Stephen was. I immediately realized that this was a great idea that would allow me to explore my interest in simulating quantum systems, while also forcing me to think hard about design and usability (something scientists aren’t known for), and that would necessitate a solid grasp of the subject matter. I began immediately, and spent the next 3 weeks working in a frenzy. You can read about the state of the project by the end of the summer school here. Suffice to say, it worked.

After the summer school I was asked to continue work on this project to extend the functionality and prepare it for ultimate inclusion in the Wolfram Language technology stack.
